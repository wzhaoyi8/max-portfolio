Concept

Latent Kiln is a computational research project that asks: can a machine learn to see ceramics the way a historian does — recognizing dynasty, region, and material culture from visual form alone?

Working from a dataset of 4,900+ ceramic vessel images sourced from the Victoria & Albert Museum archive, the project trains three distinct computer vision models, each encoding a different perceptual dimension of ceramic form. The results are aggregated into a digital exhibition that allows users to navigate the latent space of ceramic history.

Three-Model Architecture

Rather than training a single monolithic classifier, the project constructs three parallel models — each trained on a different visual representation of the same object. This reflects how human experts look at ceramics from multiple complementary perspectives simultaneously.

Training Pipeline

Each model is a fine-tuned ImageNet classifier retrained on domain-specific ceramic data. The pipeline begins with raw museum images, applies automated background removal and edge extraction, then trains with data augmentation (random rotation, crop, horizontal flip) across 15 epochs. A confidence threshold of 60% filters uncertain classifications.

The AI sorting script processes the full 4,900-image archive, routing each vessel into dynasty and regional categories. Uncertain predictions are flagged for manual review, creating a human-in-the-loop verification stage that improves training quality iteratively.

Digital Exhibition

The trained models power a browser-based digital exhibition where visitors can explore the full ceramic archive, filtered and organized by the AI's classifications. Users can also upload a sketch or photograph to receive dynasty and region predictions in real time — inverting the tool from classifier to search engine.
