Research Question

Can a building façade simultaneously act as an ecological interface, a passive climate regulator, and a data-generating performance node — without adding structural load or operational complexity?

AerroTerra proposes a ceramic module derived from Triply Periodic Minimal Surface (TPMS) geometry that turns the building skin into a living system: hosting aeroponic root structures, dissipating heat through evapotranspiration, and laying the physical groundwork for real-time building performance sensing.

TPMS Module System — Grasshopper

TPMS (Triply Periodic Minimal Surfaces) are the only geometric family that simultaneously maximises surface area, minimises material volume, and self-organises into structurally efficient lattices — making them uniquely suited to host plant root systems, manage drainage, and regulate airflow within a single ceramic element.

The parametric workflow in Grasshopper evaluates eight module variants across four performance dimensions: planting cavity volume, drainage gradient, porosity, and fabrication feasibility. Wall thickness, porosity, and curvature are modulated in real time to test behaviour across TPMS surface families.

Physical Prototyping — Slip Casting

Three TPMS surface families were physically realised through slip casting: the Schwartz P (planar, high stacking density), the Schwartz D (curved, larger planting cavities), and the Gyroid (saddle surface, maximum internal channel complexity). Each tests a distinct set of fabrication questions — mold segmentation strategy, wall thickness uniformity, and cavity geometry under ceramic shrinkage.

The modules are currently unfired greenware, awaiting bisque and glaze cycles. The next fabrication milestone is integrating a micro-mist water delivery channel into the casting process before the final glaze firing.

Site Intelligence — UAE Environmental Analysis

Abu Dhabi concentrates three compounding crises that AerroTerra directly addresses: peak summer temperatures reaching 44 °C drive HVAC cooling to 70 % of peak electricity load; near-total soil salinisation (>10,000 ppm groundwater salinity across >80 % of the emirate) makes in-ground agriculture structurally impossible; and the city's built-up fabric expands with almost no tree canopy, eliminating passive cooling from vegetation.

Performance Intelligence — Toward Digital Twins

The built-up fabric of Abu Dhabi accounts for an estimated USD 2.8 billion HVAC market by 2030 (CAGR 5.6 %). Most of that energy is wasted through poor operational decisions, not poor design — because no feedback loop connects the building skin to the systems managing it.

AerroTerra is designed from the outset as a sensor infrastructure. Each ceramic module hosts cavities sized to receive soil moisture, PM 2.5, and microclimate temperature probes. Aggregated across a façade panel, these become a real-time data mesh: a physical layer that feeds the digital twin and enables ML-driven HVAC optimisation and adaptive irrigation scheduling.

The research gap this occupies sits between computational design (Rhino / Grasshopper) and live sensor pipelines — a domain where designers trained in parametric spatial reasoning can contribute what data engineers cannot: material logic, ecological system understanding, and spatial typology.
