Concept

AerroTerra proposes a new façade typology that moves beyond enclosure — turning building skins into ecological interfaces that simultaneously support urban agriculture, regulate microclimate, and create reciprocal relationships between architecture and living systems.

At its core is a ceramic module derived from Triply Periodic Minimal Surface (TPMS) geometry. TPMS structures maximize surface area within minimal volume, enabling the module to host plant root systems, manage drainage, and perform passive climate regulation — all within a single architectural element.

Parametric Form Optimization — Grasshopper

The system was developed through an iterative parametric workflow in Grasshopper, using TPMS equations as the geometric and performative basis. Parameters including wall thickness, porosity, curvature, and module aggregation logic were continuously adjusted in response to structural behavior, planting capacity, and fabrication constraints.

Eight module variants were generated and evaluated across four plant typologies: hydroseeding moss, aeroponic crops, microgreens, and coconut fiber root structures. Each variant was assessed across axonometric, transparent, section, and plan views to verify planting viability and drainage performance.

Material Research — Ceramic Fabrication

The project is currently in the prototyping stage, testing two ceramic forming methods: slip casting and press molding. Multiple strategies of segmentation, mold design, and part assembly are being evaluated for precision, repeatability, and production feasibility.

The goal is to understand how the module behaves as a physical ceramic object through each step of the making process — identifying a practical path toward larger architectural applications.

System Integration — Façade Assembly

Individual modules aggregate into a continuous façade panel system, with mounting hardware designed for standard curtain wall integration. The assembled system supports multiple plant species simultaneously, creating a living surface that adapts seasonally to local microclimate conditions.
